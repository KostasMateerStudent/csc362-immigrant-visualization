Introduction

Our project aims to analyze the trends in nonimmigrant worker admissions to the United States over time. As an international student, Sathish has a personal interest in understanding the likelihood and trends of immigration to the US. On the other hand, Kostas' mother was an immigrant herself, giving him a unique perspective on the topic. Our project seeks to not only uncover the general trends but also investigate potential disparities within the US immigration system.

Questions

To gain deeper insights into the trends and disparities within the US immigration system, we will explore the following questions:

  • Which continents did the most nonimmigrant temporary workers come from in 2021 (workers and families)? Is it a matter of absolute population numbers or are some continents favored?
  • What are the disparities between the admitted nonimmigrants into the US? Are certain countries favored or is it a matter of absolute population numbers?
  • What was the effect of COVID-19 on the disparities between the admitted nonimmigrants into the US?
  • Where are these nonimmigrants entering the United States, and what factors contribute to their choice of destination?

Dataset

Our analysis is based on the 2021 U.S. Nonimmigrant Admissions Annual Flow Report, obtained from the US Department of Homeland Security. The dataset provides comprehensive information on nonimmigrant admissions to the United States, including detailed data gathered from Form I-94 arrival records on the number and characteristics of nonimmigrant admissions in Fiscal Year 2021. The dataset is vast and contains multiple tables, including information on the number of admissions over time, immigration status, and country of origin. In addition to the U.S. Nonimmigrant Admissions Annual Flow Report from the US Department of Homeland Security, we also used data from the World Bank to provide additional context for our analysis. Specifically, we obtained data on GDP per capita and population from the World Bank to explore potential correlations between a country's economic situation and the number of nonimmigrant temporary workers admitted to the US. By incorporating this additional data, we hoped to gain a deeper understanding of the factors that influence US immigration trends.

Visualization 1

We began our data exploration with a focus on the continents with the highest admissions, while also examining the trends over time. Our objective was to understand how population, distance, and wealth of continents contributed to the number of admissions. The study was of particular interest to Sathish, a South Asian from India, as he wanted to compare Asia's performance with other continents, especially North America and Europe. Additionally, we were keen on analyzing the impact of COVID on immigration levels globally, given that it has exacerbated immigration challenges and contributed to a more closed world. We started with an overview of the total number of non-immigrant admissions, but our interest in the worker visa category drove us to our second visualization - the number of worker admissions in the fiscal year 2021. As upperclassmen seeking employment opportunities within the US, the worker visa category's analysis was a matter of concern to us.

Visualization 2

As we delved into our analysis of worker admissions in the fiscal year 2021, we focused on comparing the worker admissions from different continents. We selected 2021 as our year of analysis since it was the first year in the post-COVID world, and we wanted to understand the impact of the pandemic on the job market. However, we acknowledged that the world had opened up significantly since then. Notably, we observed that North America had the highest number of worker admissions, likely due to the restrictions placed on countries during the pandemic. Surprisingly, Asia had more admissions than Europe, highlighting the difficulties that some continents faced in acquiring work visas and admission into the US, particularly during COVID. We were also interested in knowing where these workers entered the US to understand which regions served as entry points for nonimmigrant workers. Our analysis led us to our third visualization, a choropleth heat map of the states with the highest number of entries, which we hoped would shed more light on this topic.

Visualization 3

Our third visualization, the choropleth heat map of the states with the highest number of entries, was intriguing because it provided insights into the usual entry points for nonimmigrants into the US. We found it fascinating to be able to analyze these states in more detail and discovered that they generally had significant airports and ports, making it easier for nonimmigrants to enter. Moreover, these states were mostly high-growth areas with many businesses headquartered there. However, the visualization also raised new questions, such as the demographics of these nonimmigrants entering the US. To explore this further, we turned to our fourth visualization, which examines the gender breakdown and age demographics of nonimmigrants entering the US. This helped us gain a better understanding of the people coming into the country and the specific groups that are more likely to enter.

Visualization 4

Our fourth visualization, a population pyramid, allowed us to analyze the age and gender distribution of nonimmigrants entering the US. We were interested in exploring this to see if there were any disparities in age and gender among nonimmigrants. Our analysis revealed that the majority of nonimmigrants were either in the working age or above the retirement age, which is clearly depicted in the visual by the top and center of the pyramid being more full than the rest. Additionally, we noticed that men and women had different distributions, with men being more evenly distributed than women. This finding highlights actual disparities between men and women when it comes to being granted visas, which could be due to cultural differences between their respective countries. These disparities prompted us to question how countries and their role play into nonimmigrant numbers, leading us to create our fifth visualization, a bubble chart comparing GDP per capita and nonimmigrant admissions across countries and their populations. This helped us gain a better understanding of global disparities.

Visualization 5

Our fifth visualization was a bubble chart that explored the relationship between GDP per capita and nonimmigrant admissions. We wanted to investigate disparities between actual countries rather than just at the continent level, allowing us to gain a more detailed understanding of what is happening. This analysis was important to us because we aimed to gain a deeper understanding of the US immigration system. Our analysis revealed that Mexico had the greatest number of nonimmigrant admissions, with the majority being seasonal temporary workers. We were curious to learn where these workers were going as we prepare to join the workforce ourselves. Limitations

While working with the data, we encountered some limitations that are important to note. First, all our visualizations were based on admissions and not individual nonimmigrants. This means that the data shows the frequency with which nonimmigrants were admitted, and certain numbers could be inflated, especially for North America. For instance, a single nonimmigrant could be responsible for 730 admissions from Canada or Mexico if they lived in their home country and commuted to work every day. However, this does not necessarily mean that North America has that many more nonimmigrants than, say, Europe. Another limitation we encountered was that data from the World Bank was often unavailable for certain countries due to various reasons, such as political reasons. For example, we were unable to obtain GDP per capita data for North Korea or Taiwan, creating gaps in the data that made it impossible to plot on the bubble chart. We also only had data available from 2012-2021. Meaning we could not see larger trends from the past years. These limitations remind us of the importance of critically examining and understanding the data we work with, as it may not always provide a complete or accurate picture.

Conclusion

In conclusion, these visualizations provided us with valuable insights into nonimmigrants, nonimmigrant statuses, and disparities among people, shedding light on the US's nonimmigrant demographics. The project helped Sathish understand the multitude of variables surrounding nonimmigrant status, providing him with more informed decisions about his career path. Similarly, Kostas gained an understanding of the many different visas available for the US, many of which most Americans are not aware of. We also discovered disparities within immigration status, whether due to US institutions or other institutions abroad. Creating the bubble chart required extensive time coding, and we realized the importance of fully grasping what the data means. Adding a toggle to the graph was a useful addition as it allowed us to see how dramatically different Mexico's admission numbers were compared to the rest of the world. If we had more time, we would have explored finding more data for the absolute number of visas granted, rather than just admission numbers. Additionally, we would have investigated different visualizations, such as a radar graph, as a possibility. Further research on countries of origin, as well as the rules and regulations governing immigration, would also have been valuable. Overall, this project helped us gain a deeper understanding of nonimmigrants and immigration policies, which will be useful in our future endeavors.
