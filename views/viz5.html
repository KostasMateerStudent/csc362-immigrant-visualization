Visualization 5

Which Countries have the Highest Admission Rates into the US?

About Dataset Used for Bubble Chart:

Dataset is from Department of Homeland Security. It has the amount of admissions of temporary workers and their families in the fiscal years 2012 to 2021 and which country they are coming from. GDP per capita and population was found the IMF World Bank.

Our Question:

Looking at the bar chart and line graph in visualizations 1 and 2 we were curious to see what the trends were on a by country basis. We asked ourselves which countries had the highest admissions and why? Was it because they were richer countries? Or was it because these countries had a higher population? We also wanted to see how much COVID affected this trends.

2012

Analysis of Chart:

Looking at the logarithmic scale of the chart, it may appear that the distribution of admissions is relatively even across countries, but upon closer inspection, it becomes clear that there are some extreme outliers. Specifically, Mexico and Canada have by far the highest number of admissions into the US, which may be due to factors such as geographic proximity and strong economic ties. As we scroll through the years using the slider, we can see that there is a general uptick in the number of admissions across all countries. However, this trend is interrupted in the year 2021, which coincides with the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. Admissions dropped significantly from that year, likely due to travel restrictions and other measures put in place to mitigate the spread of the virus. It is worth noting that while the logarithmic scale of the chart makes it difficult to visually compare the magnitudes of different values, it does highlight the relative growth rates of admissions over time. In this case, we can see that the growth rate of admissions has been relatively consistent across countries, with some exceptions such as China, which saw a large increase in admissions around 2015 before leveling off in recent years. Overall, the chart provides insights into the patterns of nonimmigrant admissions into the US over time and how they may have been impacted by external factors such as the COVID-19 pandemic.

Further Discussion:

Our bubble chart visualization provides a unique perspective on the trends and disparities in nonimmigrant temporary worker admissions to the US. By examining the size and position of the bubbles, we can see which countries and regions are sending the most workers and how that has changed over time. One interesting observation from the chart is that Mexico and Canada, which are both geographically close to the US, have the highest number of admissions by far. This suggests that proximity may play a role in determining which countries are favored for nonimmigrant admissions. However, it's important to note that there are other factors at play, such as economic ties and political relationships. Another key finding from our analysis is that COVID had a significant impact on nonimmigrant admissions in 2020 and 2021. As we can see from the chart, there was a steep drop in the number of admissions during this time period, which is likely due to travel restrictions and other pandemic-related factors. Overall, our exploration of the nonimmigrant admissions dataset highlights the need for further investigation into the factors that influence who is admitted to the US for temporary work. By understanding these trends and disparities, we can work towards creating a more equitable and just immigration system for all.
